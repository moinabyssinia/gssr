1. Background

Understanding the stochastic nature of extreme water level events is important for efficient planning and design of coastal defense structures. Unfortunately, a major hurdle in the study of extreme water levels is the lack of data going back in time making it difficult to study the long-term trends and variabilities in them. As a rule of thumb, extrapolation of extreme water level events should be limited to return periods not longer than four times the available annual maximum levels. This means in order to extrapolate to a 100 year storm surge event, at least 25 years of available storm surge data are needed. This, however, is not the reality in most tide gauges as 45% of the tide gauges covered in this study have less than 25 years of data. Moreover, coastal defense structures are usually designed for a much higher return period than 100 years. Flood defenses in The Netherlands, for instance, are designed for a 10,000 year event.

2. What we present

A longer record (1836 - 2019) of quasi-global storm surge reconstruction making use of already developed data-driven models and centennial climate reanalysis data. The data-driven models employed for surge reconstruction are developed by Tadesse et al. (2020). You can download the full surge reconstruction for each dataset from the following links:

  • 20-CR Surge Reconstruction [1836 - 2015]
  • ERA-20C Surge Reconstruction [1900 - 2010]
  • ERA-Interim Surge Reconstruction [1979 - 2019]
  • MERAA-2 Reconstruction [1980 - 2019]
  • ERA-Five Reconstruction [1979 - 2019]

3. Input Datasets
  • ERA-Interim global atmospheric reanalysis,
  • ERA-20C global atmospheric reanalysis,
  • 20CRV3 (NOAA-CIRES) atmospheric reanalysis,
  • MERRA-2 atmospheric reanalysis,
  • ERA-Five atmospheric reanalysis,
  • Global Extreme Sea Level Analysis - Sea-level Data
4. Methodology

The methodology for this study builds on what is implemented by Tadesse et al. (2020).
